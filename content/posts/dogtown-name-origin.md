The popular version of the story is more interesting than the true one, which is often how it goes.

The popular version: In 1904, the World's Fair brought Igorot tribespeople from the Philippines to Forest Park as living exhibits. The Igorots, according to neighborhood legend, developed a taste for the dogs of the surrounding area, wandering into nearby yards and helping themselves. The neighborhood, traumatized and short several pets, became known forever after as Dogtown.

The only problem with this tale, aside from its racist undertones, is that it's absolutely and totally false, says Bob Corbett, founder of the Dogtown Historical Society. A newspaper article referencing "Dogtown" was found dated August 14, 1889 — many years before any Igorots set foot in Missouri.

The real explanation is less colorful but more interesting if you think about it long enough. A "doghole" is a small opening from one place in a coal mine to another, or a small mine employing fewer than fifteen miners. St. Louis's Dogtown was home to countless clay and coal mines. The Irish immigrants who worked those mines settled nearby. They brought their church with them — St. James the Greater, which still stands at Tamm and Wade, dating to 1860.

The Igorot story will never die. It's too good. It has dogs, an exotic villain, and a neighborhood name that suddenly makes sense. The true story has miners and etymology. One of these is a better story. The other one is true.

Dogtown is still there, still Irish, still throwing one of the best St. Patrick's Day parades in the country. The mines are long gone. The dogholes are filled. The name remains.
