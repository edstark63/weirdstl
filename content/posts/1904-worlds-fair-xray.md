There is something quietly extraordinary about the fact that millions of people wandered through Forest Park in 1904 and came home having seen, for the first time in their lives, the inside of a human hand.

The X-ray machine was successfully exhibited at the 1904 World's Fair — one of dozens of things the Louisiana Purchase Exposition introduced to a world that had no idea what was coming. Among the scientific wizardry introduced that year were the baby incubator, the electric typewriter, and the telautograph — an early version of the fax machine. But it was the X-ray that stopped people cold.

Think about what that meant. You handed a stranger your hand, and they showed you your skeleton. You were alive and you were looking at your own bones. Your wedding ring floated there around something that was you, but not quite you yet. Wilhelm Röntgen had only discovered X-rays nine years earlier. Thomas Edison had refined the machine. And now here it was, on the Pike, between the camel rides and the ice cream cones.

The fair's purpose, in the words of its chief executive, was to demonstrate that human history had reached its "apotheosis" in Forest Park. That's a bold claim for a city to make. But standing in a room where you can see through your own skin, it must have felt almost believable.

The fairgrounds covered 1,200 acres. Nearly 19.7 million people attended. Most of what was built was torn down when it was over. The Art Museum stayed. The Zoo's flight cage stayed. And the knowledge that the human body was not as solid or as secret as anyone had assumed — that stayed too.
