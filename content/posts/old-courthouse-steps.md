You have almost certainly walked past it. It sits in the shadow of the Arch, a domed building that tourists photograph as a backdrop. It looks like a temple to justice. It was, for a while, also something else.

Between 1839 and 1862, the court sold more than 500 enslaved men, women, and children on the steps of the Old Courthouse. The court organized property sales when people went bankrupt or died without a will — and enslaved people were considered property, auctioned alongside furniture and farmland.

Before the auctions, appraisers inspected and sometimes assaulted enslaved people while determining how much they could be sold for. During sales, enslaved people were often forced to be naked or wear very little clothing in front of large audiences. Many auctions resulted in the breaking up of enslaved families.

Nearby, Bernard Lynch operated the most prominent slave pen in St. Louis — a holding facility at 5th and Myrtle streets where enslaved men, women, and children were kept in cramped, prison-like cells before being auctioned. Lynch advertised widely. Business was good.

The practice ended not with a law but with a crowd. On New Year's Day 1861, some 2,000 hecklers shouted down auctioneers at a public sale on the steps of the Old Courthouse, stopping the practice for good in St. Louis. Four years later, Missouri abolished slavery entirely.

Inside that same building, Dred and Harriet Scott had filed suit for their freedom in 1846. The Old Courthouse was the site of over 300 suits for freedom — people arguing, through the courts, for the right to be human. Most of them lost. The building heard both things at once: people being sold on the steps, people pleading for liberty inside.

It reopened after renovations in May 2025. It is part of Gateway Arch National Park now. You can walk right up to those steps.
